üõ°Ô∏è Cyberbullying Guide for Parents

Everything you need to know to protect and support your teenager

Understanding Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying is the use of digital technologies to repeatedly and intentionally harm others. Unlike traditional bullying, cyberbullying:

  • Happens 24/7, not just during school hours
  • Can reach unlimited audiences instantly
  • Creates permanent digital evidence
  • Allows for anonymous harassment
  • Follows victims into their homes

Common Forms of Cyberbullying

  • Harassment: Sending cruel, vicious, or threatening messages
  • Denigration: Spreading rumors or sharing information to damage someone's reputation
  • Flaming: Online fighting using angry and vulgar language
  • Impersonation: Creating fake accounts to damage someone's reputation
  • Outing and trickery: Sharing someone's private information or embarrassing content
  • Exclusion: Deliberately excluding someone from online groups
  • Cyberstalking: Repeated harassment that includes threats of physical harm

üëÅÔ∏è Recognizing the Warning Signs

Many teenagers don't immediately tell their parents about cyberbullying. Watch for these warning signs:

Digital Behavior Changes

  • Suddenly avoiding or appearing upset when using devices
  • Hiding screens when you enter the room
  • Deleting social media accounts or apps
  • Receiving calls or messages that cause distress
  • Being secretive about online activities

Emotional and Social Changes

  • Increased anxiety, depression, or irritability
  • Withdrawal from family and friends
  • Loss of interest in activities they previously enjoyed
  • Changes in sleep patterns or appetite
  • Unexplained mood swings

Trust Your Instincts

If you notice several of these signs, especially in combination, trust your parental instincts and start a gentle conversation with your teenager.

ü§ù How to Respond When Your Teen Opens Up

Your initial response when your teenager tells you about cyberbullying will determine whether they continue to trust you with difficult situations.

What TO Do

  • Listen actively without interrupting or immediately offering solutions
  • Thank them for trusting you with this information
  • Validate their feelings and acknowledge their courage in telling you
  • Ask questions to understand the full scope of the situation
  • Work together to document evidence and plan next steps
  • Follow up regularly to check on their wellbeing

What NOT to Do

  • Don't take away their devices as a punishment or "solution"
  • Don't minimize the situation by saying "just ignore it" or "kids will be kids"
  • Don't contact the bully or their parents directly without involving school authorities
  • Don't share their story with others without their permission
  • Don't blame them for what happened or question their actions

Documenting Evidence Together

Evidence Collection Checklist:

  • Screenshot all harmful messages, posts, or comments
  • Save URLs and usernames of perpetrators
  • Document dates, times, and platforms used
  • Keep a written log of incidents and their impact
  • Save any relevant emails or other digital communications

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention and Protection Strategies

Privacy Settings Checklist

  • Set social media profiles to private
  • Turn off location sharing
  • Limit who can send friend requests or follow
  • Restrict who can see posts and personal information
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Review and clean up friend/follower lists regularly

Building Digital Resilience

Help your teenager develop these essential skills:

  • Critical thinking: Question the accuracy and intent of online content
  • Empathy: Consider how their words might affect others
  • Communication skills: Express themselves clearly and respectfully
  • Self-regulation: Manage emotions before responding online
  • Digital citizenship: Understand rights and responsibilities online

üìû When to Seek Professional Help

Consider professional mental health support if your teenager shows:

  • Persistent depression or anxiety
  • Significant changes in behavior or personality
  • Declining academic performance
  • Social withdrawal or isolation
  • Sleep or appetite disturbances
  • Expressions of hopelessness or self-harm

üåü Cyberbullying Survival Guide for Teens

You're not alone in this - here's how to take control and stay strong

ü§ù You're Not Alone in This

If you're dealing with cyberbullying, know that it's NOT your fault, and there are people who want to help. This guide gives you the tools, resources, and strategies you need to handle cyberbullying like a boss.

Fact: 1 in 3 Canadian teens experience cyberbullying. You're definitely not alone in this.

üé≠ What Even IS Cyberbullying?

The Real Definition:

Cyberbullying is when someone repeatedly uses technology (social media, texts, gaming platforms, etc.) to hurt, threaten, embarrass, or target someone else. It's basically being a jerk online, but with serious consequences.

ü§î Why Does This Happen?

Some people cyberbully because:

  • They feel powerful hiding behind a screen
  • They're dealing with their own problems and taking it out on others
  • They think there won't be consequences online
  • They're trying to fit in with a certain group
  • They're copying behavior they've seen elsewhere

üì± Different Types of Online Drama

Harassment

Sending mean messages, comments, or threats repeatedly

Exclusion

Purposely leaving someone out of group chats, games, or online activities

Doxing

Sharing someone's private info (address, phone number) without permission

Impersonation

Pretending to be someone else online to damage their reputation

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning Signs to Watch For

You Might Be Getting Cyberbullied If:

  • You're getting mean messages, comments, or emails
  • People are spreading rumors about you online
  • Your photos are being shared without permission
  • You're being excluded from group chats or online games
  • Someone is pretending to be you online
  • You're feeling anxious every time you check your phone
  • You're avoiding social media or certain apps

‚ú® It's NOT Your Fault

Listen up: Whatever happened, whatever they're saying about you, whatever you posted or didn't post - cyberbullying is NEVER your fault. The person choosing to be cruel is 100% responsible for their actions, not you.

üö® What to Do RIGHT NOW

üõë Stop & Breathe

  • Don't respond when you're angry or upset
  • Take a break from your phone/computer
  • Talk to someone you trust
  • Remember: this isn't your fault

üì∏ Document Everything

  • Screenshot all mean messages/posts
  • Save URLs and usernames
  • Write down dates and times
  • Don't delete anything yet

üì¢ Report It

  • Use the platform's reporting tools
  • Tell a trusted adult (parent, teacher, counselor)
  • Contact school if it involves classmates
  • Call police if there are threats of violence

ü§ó Get Support

  • Talk to friends and family
  • Reach out to a counselor
  • Call a helpline (numbers below)
  • Join a support group

üõ°Ô∏è Staying Safe Online

Lock Down Your Privacy

  • Make your social media accounts private
  • Turn off location sharing
  • Review your friends and followers regularly
  • Think before you post - would you be okay if your grandma saw this?
  • Don't share personal information like your address or phone number

Being a Good Digital Citizen

  • Treat others the way you want to be treated online
  • Think before you comment or share
  • Stand up for others who are being bullied (safely)
  • Report content that seems harmful or illegal
  • Remember: there are real people behind usernames

üí™ Remember You're Stronger Than You Think

Cyberbullying is tough, but you're tougher. You have the power to:

  • Control your digital spaces
  • Choose who gets access to you
  • Build supportive relationships
  • Stand up for yourself and others
  • Get help when you need it
